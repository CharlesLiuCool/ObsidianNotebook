>**Definition.** Let ${\underline{x} = (x_1, x_2, ..., x_n)}$ and ${\underline{y} = (y_1, ..., y_n) \in \Bbb{R}^n}$.
>We define the *inner product*:
>${\underline{x} \cdot \underline{y} := \sum\limits_{k=1}^n x_ky_k}$
>and the *norm*:
>${||x|| = (\underline{x} \cdot \underline{x})^{1/2} = (\sum\limits_{k=1}^nx_k^2)^{1/2}}$
>
>The norm ${||x - y||}$ is called the distance between ${\underline{x}}$ and ${\underline{y}}$.

>**Theorem.** Let ${\underline{x}, \underline{y} \in \Bbb{R}^n}$. Then we have
>1. ${||\underline{x}|| \ge 0}$, and ${||\underline{x}|| = 0 \iff \underline{x} = 0}$
>2. For ${a \in \Bbb{R}}$, ${||a\underline{x}|| = |a|\;\,||\underline{x}||}$
>3. ${||\underline{x} - \underline{y}|| = || \underline{y} - \underline{x}||}$
>4. Cauchy-Schwarz inequality: ${||\underline{x} \cdot \underline{y} || \le ||\underline{x} || \;||\underline{y}||}$
>5. Triangle inequality: 
>   ${||\underline{x} + \underline{y}|| \le ||\underline{x}|| + ||\underline{y}||}$
>   and
>   ${\left| ||\underline{x}|| - ||\underline{y}|| \right| \le || \underline{x} - \underline{y} ||}$

(Hint) ${(5)}$ follows from ${(4)}$.
${\begin{align} ||\underline{x} + \underline{y}||^2 &= (\underline{x} + \underline{y}) (\underline{x} + \underline{y}) \\ &= ||\underline{x}||^2 + 2\underline{x}\underline{y} + ||\underline{y}||^2 \\ &\le ||\underline{x}||^2 + 2||\underline{x}||\,||\underline{y}||+||\underline{y}||^2 \\ &= (||\underline{x}||+ ||\underline{y}||)^2\end{align}}$

## **Open Balls and Open Sets**

Let ${\underline{a} \in \Bbb{R}^n, r > 0}$ in ${\Bbb{R}}$
The open ball of radius ${r}$ and center ${\underline{a}}$ is ${B(\underline{a}, r) := \{\underline{x} \in \Bbb{R}^n \,|\, ||\underline{x} - \underline{a}|| < r\}}$

![[1. Open Sets Graphic 1]]

>**Definition** Let ${S \subseteq \Bbb{R}^n}$
>${\underline{a} \in S}$ is called an *interior point* of ${S}$ if there exists ${r > 0}$ such that ${B(\underline{a},r) \subseteq S}$.

The set of all interior points of ${S}$ is called the *interior* of ${S}$, denoted by ${\text{int}(S)}$.

>**Definition** ${S\subseteq \Bbb{R}^n}$ is called an open set if all its points are interior points.

**Example** In ${\Bbb{R}}$,
${(a,b)}$ is open.
${[a,b]}$ is *not* open ${\text{int}([a,b]) = (a,b)}$
${[a,b)}$ is *not* open
${\Bbb{R}}$ is open.
${\emptyset}$ is open.

**Example** In ${\Bbb{R}^2}$,
${(a_1,b_2) \times (a_2,b_2)}$ is open.
${\Bbb{R} \times \{0\}}$ (i.e. the ${x}$-axis) is *not* open.

![[1. Open Sets Graphic 2]]

>**Theorem.** The union of any collection of open sets is open.
>*Proof:* Let ${\{A_i \,|\,i \in \wedge\}}$ be a collection of open sets.
>Let ${S = \bigcup\limits_{i\in\wedge}A_i}$.
>For any ${a \in S}$, ${a \in A_i}$ for some ${i \in \wedge}$.
>Since ${A_i}$ is open, ${\exists\;r > 0}$ such that ${B(a,r) \subseteq A_i \subseteq S}$.
>i.e. ${a}$ is an interior point of ${S}$.
>Thus, ${S}$ is open.

>**Theorem.** Any *finite* intersection of open sets is open.
>*Proof:* Let ${A_1,A_2,...,A_n}$ be open sets.
>Let ${S = \bigcap\limits_{i=1}^nA_i}$
>If ${S = \emptyset}$, then ${S}$ is open.
>Assume ${S \neq \emptyset}$
>For any ${a \in S}$, ${a \in A_i}$ for all ${i=1,2,...,n}$.
>Since ${A_i}$ is open, there exists ${r_i > 0}$ such that ${B(a,r_i) \subseteq A_i}$ for each ${i=1,...,n}$.
>Let ${r = \min\{r_1,r_2,...,r_n\}}$.
>Then ${B(a,r) \subseteq B(a,r_i)}$ for all ${i=1,...,n}$.
>${\implies B(a,r) \subseteq \bigcap\limits_{i=1}^nA_i = S}$.
>Thus ${S}$ is open.

*Remark:* Arbitrary intersection of open sets may *NOT* be open.
e.g. ${\bigcap\limits_{n=1}^\infty (-\frac{1}{n}, \frac{1}{n}) = \{0\}}$ is *not* an open set.

## **The Structure of Open Sets in ${\Bbb{R}}$**

>**Definition** Let ${S\subseteq \Bbb{R}}$ be open.
>An open interval ${I}$ (which may be finite or infinite) is called a *component interval* of ${S}$ if ${I \subseteq S}$ and there is no open interval ${J}$ such that ${I \subsetneq J \subset S}$.

>**Theorem.** Let ${S \subseteq \Bbb{R}}$ be open, and ${S \neq \emptyset}$.
>Then every point of ${S}$ belongs to exactly one component interval of ${S}$.

(Hint) Assume ${x \in S}$
Since ${S}$ is open, ${x \in I \subseteq S}$ for some interval ${I}$. We should choose the largest interval satisfying this.
That is, ${I_x = (a(x),  b(x))}$
where 
${a(x) = \inf\{a:(a,x) \subseteq S\}}$
${b(x) = \sup\{b: (x,b) \subseteq S\}}$

>**Theorem.** Let ${S \neq \emptyset \subseteq \Bbb{R}}$ be open
>Then ${S}$ is the union of a countable collection of disjoint component intervals of ${S}$.

(Hint) For each ${x \in S}$, there exists a component interval ${I_x}$ of ${S}$ such that ${x \in I_x}$.
*Note:* For ${x_1,x_2 \in S}$, if ${I_{x_1} \cap I_{x_2} \neq \emptyset}$, then ${I_{x_1} = I_{x_2}}$
Thus, ${S = \bigcup\limits_{i\in\wedge}I_i}$, ${I_i}$ are disjoint component intervals of ${S}$.
Since each ${I_i}$ contains a rational point and ${\Bbb{Q}}$ is countable, ${\wedge}$ is countable.
