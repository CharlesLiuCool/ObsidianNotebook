>**Definition** Primitive $n$-th Root of Unity
>A $n$-th root of unity $\zeta$ is *primitive* is not a root of unity for any smaller positive power. Specifically, it is primitive if
>$$\zeta^k \neq 1 \; \forall\; 1 \le k < n$$
>Equivalently, $\zeta = e^{2\pi i k /n}$ is primitive if $\gcd(k,n) = 1$

>**Definition** Power Basis
>Let $K = \Bbb{Q}(\alpha)$ be the number field, generated by a single algebraic element (that is, the smallest field containing both $\Bbb{Q}$ and $\alpha$)
>The power basis of $K$ is:
>$$(1, \alpha, \alpha^2,...,\alpha^{n-1})$$
>Every element $x \in K$ can be written *uniquely* as a linear combination of these powers
>$$x = a_0 + a_1\alpha, a_2\alpha^2 + ... + \alpha_{n-1}\alpha^{n-1}$$
>where $a_i \in \Bbb{Q}$ (or $a_i$ in $\Bbb{Z}$ if we're in the ring of integers).

>**Definition** Ring Expansion Factor
>Let $\mathcal{R}$ be a ring. The ring expansion factor is defined as
>$$\gamma_{\mathcal{R}} := \max_{a,b \in \mathcal{R}} \frac{||a \cdot b ||} {||a|| \cdot ||b||}$$
>Intuitively, this represents the worst possible factor of the coefficient size when you multiply two ring elements and reduce by the chosen power basis.
>Equivalently, this means for all $x,y \in \mathcal{R}$,
>$$||x \cdot y|| \le \gamma_\mathcal{R} ||x||\,||y||$$

>**Definition** Vandermonde Matrix
>Given an *ordered set of elements*
>$$T = (r_0, r_1,...,r_{t-1})$$
>from some field (or ring), the *Vandermonde matrix* associated to $T$ is
>$$V_T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & ... & 1 \\ r_0 & r_1 & ... &r_{t-1} \\ r_0^2 & r_1^2 & ... & r_{t-1}^2 \\ \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ r_0^{t-1} & r_1^{t-1} & ... & r_{t-1}^{t-1} \end{pmatrix}$$
>where each column corresponds to one element $r_j$, and each row gives an increasing power of those elements.
>

### **3.2 Quantum Information**

>**Definition** Quantum State
>A vector $| \psi \rangle$ in a complex Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$ with $\|\,|\psi\rangle \| = 1$. (In Russell Lai's paper, $\mathcal{H}$ is finite dimensional)

>**Definition** Hermitian Operator
>Let $\mathcal{H}$ be a complex Hilbert space, and let
>$A: \mathcal{H} \to \mathcal{H}$ be a linear operator.
>
>Then $A$ is a Hermitian if
>$$A = A^{\dagger}$$
>where $A^\dagger$ (the *adjoint* of $A$, or the conjugate transpose) is defined by the condition
>$$\langle x,Ay\rangle = \langle A^{\dagger}x, y \rangle \text{ for all }x,y \in \mathcal{H}$$
>So $A$ is Hermitian if
>$$\langle x, Ay \rangle = \langle Ax, y \rangle \; \forall\;x,y \in \mathcal{H}$$
>
>*For More Intuition:* [[Hermitian Operators]]

>**Definition** Space of Hermitian Operators 
> We denote $S(\mathcal{H})$ as the space of Hermitian operators on $\mathcal{H}$

>**Definition** Mixed State
>A mixed state describes a quantum system that is not in a definite pure state, but rather a probabilistic mixture of several possible pure states.
> 
> Suppose a system can be in pure state $| \psi_i \rangle$ with classical probability $p_i$ (where $p_i \ge 0$ and $\sum\limits_{i} p_i = 1$)
> The system's *mixed state* is represented by the *density matrix*
> $$\rho = \sum\limits_i p_i |\psi_i \rangle \langle \psi_i |$$
> *For More Intuition:* [[Mixed State Probability Density Matrix]]


>**Definition** Density Matrix
>A positive semidefinite operator $\rho \in S(\mathcal{H})$ with $\text{Tr}(\rho) = 1$  is called a *density matrix*. It represents a probabilistic mixture of pure states (a mixed state).
>- *Positive Semidefinite:* $\langle\phi|\rho|\phi\rangle \ge 0$ for all $|\phi \rangle \in \mathcal{H}$, which ensures probabilities computed using $\rho$ are always non-negative
>- *$\text{Tr}(\rho) = 1$*: ensures total probability = $1$
>
>The density matrix corresponding to the pure state $|\psi\rangle$ is $|\psi \rangle \langle \psi|$.

>**Definition** Registers
>A *register* is a subsystem of a larger quantum system, a "chunk" of the total Hilbert space.
> 
> If the total system has Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$ and you think of it as composed of two smaller systems, then we write
> $$\mathcal{H} = \mathcal{H_1} \otimes \mathcal{H_2}$$
> where 
> $\cdot\;\;\mathcal{H_1}$ is the Hilbert space of register $1$
> $\cdot\;\;\mathcal{H_2}$ is the Hilbert space of register $2$
>
> We sometimes write $\rho^{\mathcal{H}_i}$ to specify that $\rho \in S(\mathcal{H}_i)$.
>
> *For More Intuition:* [[Registers in Hilbert Spaces]]

>**Definition** Unitary Operation
>A *unitary operation* is a complex square matrix $U$ such that $UU^\dagger = \textbf{I}$. The operation $U$ transforms the pure state $| \psi \rangle$ to the pure state $U |\psi\rangle$, and the density matrix $\rho$ to the density matrix $U\rho U^{\dagger}$.
>
>We write $U(\mathcal{H})$ for the set of unitary operators on $\mathcal{H}$
>
>*For More Intuition:* [[Unitary Operation]]

>**Definition** Projector
>A *projector* $\Pi$ is a Hermitian operator ($\Pi^{\dagger} = \Pi$) such that $\Pi^2 = \Pi$.
>*For More Intuition:* [[Projectors and Projective Measurements in Hilbert Spaces]]

>**Definition** Projective Measurement
>A *projective measurement* $P = (\Pi_i)_{i \in S}$ (where $S$ is the set of all possible measurement outcomes) such that $\sum\limits_{i \in S} \Pi_i = \textbf{I}$. this implies that $\Pi_i\Pi_j = 0$ for distinct $i$ and $j$ in $S$.

The application of $P$ to a pure state $| \psi \rangle$ yields outcome $i \in S$ with probability $p_i = ||\Pi_i | \psi \rangle||^2$.

In this case the *post-measurement state* is $|\psi_i\rangle = \Pi_i |\psi \rangle / \sqrt{p_i}$.
When you obtain outcome $i$, the wavefunction collapses into the part of $|\psi\rangle$, creating a new unnormalized post-measurement state $\Pi_i|\psi\rangle$

To normalize, we see the norm squared is exactly $p_i$
$$\langle \psi | \Pi_i^\dagger \Pi_i | \psi \rangle = \langle \psi|\Pi_i |\psi\rangle = p_i$$

(Recall probability of obtaining outcome $i$ is $p_i = \langle \psi | \Pi_i | \psi \rangle$)

So after normalization, we get
$$|\psi_i \rangle = \dfrac{\Pi_i |\psi\rangle}{\sqrt{p_i}}$$

>**Theorem**
>A state $|\psi\rangle$ is an *eigenstate* of $P$ if it is an eigenstate of every $\Pi_i$.

>**Definition** Binary Projective Measurement
>A two-outcome projective measurement is called a *binary projective measurement* and is written as $P = (\Pi, \textbf{I}- \Pi)$, where $\Pi$ is associated with the outcome $a$ and $\textbf{I} - \Pi$ with the outcome $0$.

>**Definition** Completely-Positive Trace-Preserving (CPTP) map
>General (non-unitary) evolution of a quantum state can be represented via a *completely-positive trace-preserving* (CPTP) map $T: S(\mathcal{H}) \to S(\mathcal{H}')$
>
>It satisfies the properties that it is *trace-preserving,* which means for every $\rho \in S(\mathcal{H})$, it holds that $\text{Tr}(T(\rho)) = \text{Tr}(\rho)$, and *linear*.
> 
> *For More Intuition:* [[CPTP and General Evolution]]

For every CPTP map $T$: $S(\mathcal{H}) \to S(\mathcal{H})$, there exists a *unitary dilation* that operates on an expanded Hilbert space $\mathcal{H} \otimes \mathcal{K}$, so that $\text{T}(\rho) = \text{Tr}_{\mathcal{K}}(U(\rho \otimes |0\rangle \langle 0|^{\mathcal{K}})U^{\dagger}$). This is *not necessarily unique*.


If $T$ is described as a circuit then there is a dilation $U_T$ represented by a circuit of size $O(|T|)$

>**Definition** Partial Trace
>Let $\rho_{AB}$ be a density operator on $\mathcal{H}_A \otimes \mathcal{H}_B$.
>The partial trace over $B$ is the unique map
>$$\text{Tr}_B: S(\mathcal{H}_A \otimes \mathcal{H}_B) \to S(\mathcal{H}_A)$$
>such that for product states:
>$$\text{Tr}_B(\rho_A \otimes \rho_B) = (\text{Tr}\rho_B) \rho_A$$
>
>*For More Intuition:* [[Partial Trace]]

For Hilbert spaces $\mathcal{A},\mathcal{B}$, the *partial trace* over $\mathcal{B}$ is the unique CPTP map $\text{Tr}_{\mathcal{B}}: S(A \otimes B) \to S(\mathcal{A})$ such that $\text{Tr}_{\mathcal{B}}(\rho_A \otimes \rho_B) = \text{Tr}(\rho_B)\rho_A$ for every $\rho_A \in S(\mathcal{A})$ and $\rho_B \in S(\mathcal{B})$.

*For More Intuition*: [[Stinespring (Unitary) Dilation Theorem]]

>**Definition** General Measurement
>A *general measurement* is a CPTP map $M: S(\mathcal{H}) \to S(\mathcal{H} \otimes \mathcal{O})$ where $\mathcal{O}$ is an ancilla register holding a classical outcome. Specifically, given measurement operators $\{M_i\}_{i=1}^N$ such that $\sum\limits_{i=1}^n M_i^\dagger M_i = \bf{I}$ and a basis $\{|i\rangle\}_{i=1}^N$ for $\mathcal{O}$, $$M(\rho) = \sum\limits_{i=1}^N(M_i\rho M_i^\dagger \otimes |i\rangle\langle i|^\mathcal{O})$$
>A measurement induces a probability distribution over its outcomes given by $$\text{Pr}[i] = \text{Tr}(|i\rangle\langle i|^{\mathcal{O}}M(\rho))$$
>We denote sampling from this distribution by
>$$i \in M(\rho)$$
>*For More Intuition:* [[General Measurements]]
>Russell Lai sometimes implicitly discards the outcome register
>
>*Note*: Russell Lai writes $\sum\limits_{i=1}^n M_i M_i^{\dagger} = \bf{I}$, which is WRONG! That's the Heisenberg interpretation, but later he switches back to SchrÃ¶dinger. Trace is cyclic, not commutative!

A *projective measurement* is a general measurement where the $M_i$ are projectors.

>**Definition** Trace Distance
>The *trace distance* between states $\rho$, $\sigma$, denoted $d(\rho,\sigma)$, is defined as
>$$d(\rho,\sigma) = \frac{1}{2}\text{Tr}\left(\sqrt{(\rho-\sigma)^2}\right)$$
>*For More Intuition*: [[Trace Distance]]

>**Theorem** Trace Distance under CPTP Maps is Contractive
>The trace distance is *contractive* under CPTP maps. That means for any CPTP map $T$, $$d(T(\rho), T(\sigma)) \le d(\rho, \sigma)$$ It follows that for any measurement $M$, the statistical distance between the distributions $M(\rho)$ and $M(\sigma)$ is bounded by $d(\rho, \sigma)$.

>**Definition** Quantum Computational Distinguishability.
>For states $\rho, \sigma$,
>$$d_{\text{comp}} := \max_{D,|D| \le N} |\text{Pr}[D(\rho) \to 1] - \text{Pr}[D(\sigma)]\to 1|$$
> where $D$ is a quantum circuit.
