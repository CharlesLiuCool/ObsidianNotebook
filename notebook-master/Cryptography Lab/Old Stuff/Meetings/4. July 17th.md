We can also use direct reduction, consider the following.

>**Theorem.** 
Let ${G: \{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}^l}$,${l(n) > n}$.
Suppose $X_1, X_2, ...,X_n$ over $X_1,X_2,X_n$ over $\{0,1\}^l$ and $X_i \underset{c}{\approx}X_{i+1}\;\forall\;1 \le i \le n-1$, then $X_i \underset{c}{\approx} X_n$ 

*Proof:*
We will prove the contrapositive
Assume ${X_i \not\underset{c}{\approx} X_n}$ for PPT adversary. Then $|\Pr[D(X_n) = 1] - \Pr[D(X_i) = 1]| = \epsilon$, $\epsilon$ non-negligible.

We can use telescoping sum. Take $\large{i = 0}$
$\begin{align} &\left|\Pr[D(X_n) = 1] - \Pr[D(X_0) = 1]\right| \\ &= \left| \sum_{i=1}^n \Pr[D(X_i) = 1] - \Pr[D(X_{i-1}) = 1] \right| \\ &\leq \sum_{i=1}^n \left| \Pr[D(X_i) = 1] - \Pr[D(X_{i-1}) = 1] \right| \end{align}$

By pigeonhole, for some $\large{j \in [i,n]}$, the distinguisher breaks if $\large{\epsilon}$ is non-negligible.
