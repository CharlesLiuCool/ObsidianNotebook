Let ${A =}$ an ${m \times n}$ matrix with column vectors ${a_1, a_2, ... a_n \in \mathbb{R}^m}$ 

>${x = a}$ (column) vector in ${\mathbb{R}}$

$${A_{m \times n} x_{n\times1} =\;x_1a_1\;+\;x_2a_2\;+ ...\;+\;x_na_n \in \mathbb{R}^m}$$
We can view this as a transformation or a mapping from ${\mathbb{R}^n}$ to ${\mathbb{R}^m}$ 

$${\mathbb{R}^n \xrightarrow{\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;}\mathbb{R}^m}$$
$${x \xrightarrow{\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;} Ax}$$
### Definition 1.

A *transformation* (or a mapping) ${T: \mathbb{R}^n \xrightarrow{} \mathbb{R}^m}$ is a rule that assigns a vector T(x) in ${\mathbb{R}^m}$ for every vector ${x \in \mathbb{R}^n}$.

$${T: \mathbb{R}^n \xrightarrow{} \mathbb{R}^m}$$
$${\underbrace{x}_{\text{domain of T}} \xrightarrow{} \underbrace{T(x)}_{\text{codomain of T}}}$$
We call T(x) the *image* of x.

The set of all images is called the *range* of T, i.e.,
$${\text{Rang}(T) = \{T(x): \text{for all x in the domain}\}}$$
Clearly,

$${\text{Rang}(T) \subset \text{codomain of }T}$$

___
### Question

**(1)** Given ${b}$ in the codomain ${\mathbb{R}^m}$, is there ${x \in \mathbb{R}^m}$ such that ${T(x) = b?}$

**(2)** Is it possible for two different inputs in the domain to give the same output?

### Definition 2.

Transformation ${T: \mathbb{R}^n \xrightarrow{} \mathbb{R}^m}$ is called a linear transformation if 
1. ${T(u+v) = T(u) + T(v)}$ for all ${u}$ and ${v}$ in ${\mathbb{R}^n}$.
2. ${T(cu) = cT(u)}$ for all ${u} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and ${c \in \mathbb{R}}$

### Theorem 1 (Matrix Transformation).

Suppose ${T: \mathbb{R}^n \xrightarrow{} \mathbb{R}^m}$ defined by ${T(x) = Ax}$ where ${A}$ is an ${m \times n}$ matrix. Then ${T}$ is a linear transformation.

### Proof. 

By the linear properties of matrix multiplication,
$${T(u+v) = T(u) + T(v)}$$ for all ${u}$ and ${v}$ in ${\mathbb{R}^n}$.
 $${T(u + v) = A(u + v) = Au + Av = T(u) + T(v)}$$
 $${T(cu) = A(cu) = c(Au) = cT(u)}$$
 for all ${u}$ and ${v \in \mathbb{R}^n}$ and ${c \in \mathbb{R}}$. This shows that this ${T}$ is a linear transformation.

### Example 1.

Let ${A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -3 \\ 3 & 5 \\ -1 & 7 \end{bmatrix}}$ Let ${T}$: ${\mathbb{R}^2 \xrightarrow{} \mathbb{R}^3}$ given by ${T(x) = Ax}$.

Let

$${u = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix},\;\;\;\;b = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \\ -5 \end{bmatrix}, \;\;\;\; z = \begin{bmatrix} \,3\, \\ 2 \\ 5\end{bmatrix}}$$
1. Find the image of ${u}$, ${T(u)}$.
2. Find ${x \in \mathbb{R}^2}$ such that ${T(x) = b}$.
3. Is there more than one ${x} \in \mathbb{R}^2$ whose image under ${T}$ is ${b}$?
4. Is ${z}$ in the image of ${T}$ ?

#### Solution:

1. ${T(u) = Au = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -3 \\ 3 & 5 \\ -1 & 7\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 7 \times 2 + (-3) \times (-1) \\ 3 \times 2 + 5 \times (-1) \\ (-1) \times 2 + 7 \times (-1) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ 1 \\ -9 \end{bmatrix}}$ 
2. Find ${x \in \mathbb{R}^2}$ such that ${T(x) = b}$

>**NOTE:** ${T(x) = Ax}$

We need to solve the augmented matrix of ${Ax = b}$

$${\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -3 & 3 \\ 3 & 5 & 2 \\ -1 & 7 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow [\huge{R_3\;\rightarrow\;R_3\;+\;R_1}]{\huge{R_2\;\rightarrow\;R_2\;+\;R_1\times(-3)}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -3 & 3 \\ 0 & 14 & -7 \\ 0 & 4 & -2 \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow {\huge{R_2\;\rightarrow\;R_2\times(\frac{1}{14})}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -3 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 & -\frac{1}{2} \\ 0 & 4 & -2\end{bmatrix}  }$$
$${\xrightarrow{\huge{R_3\;\rightarrow\;R_3\;+\;R_2\times(-4)}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -3 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 & -\frac{1}{2} \\ 0 & 0 & 0\end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{\huge{R_1\;\rightarrow\;R_1\;+\;R_2\times3}} \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & \frac{3}{2} \\ 0 & 1 & -\frac{1}{2} \end{bmatrix}}_{\huge{\text{REF!}}}}$$
Thus, ${x_1 = \frac{3}{2}}$ and ${x_2 = -\frac{1}{2}}$; i.e, ${x = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{3}{2} \\ -\frac{1}{2} \end{bmatrix}}$
3. There is only *one solution* ${x \in \mathbb{R}^2}$ such that ${T(x) = b}$. This is because every column except the rightmost column of the augmented matrix(\[A | b]) has a *pivot*. In this case, ${Ax = b}$ is *consistent* and has a *unique solution*.

4. If ${z}$ is in the image of ${T}$, then there exists ${x \in \mathbb{R}^2}$ such that $${Ax = z}$$ Augmented matrix of ${Ax = z}$ is ${\begin{bmatrix} 1 & -3 & 3 \\ 3 & 5 & 2 \\ -1 & 7 & 5 \end{bmatrix}}$ . $$$$By ERO, we find its EF. $${\begin{bmatrix} \fbox{1} & -3 & 3 \\ 0 & \fbox{1} & -\frac{1}{2} \\ 0 & 0 & \fbox{10}\; \end{bmatrix}}$$ Since the rightmost column of the augmented matrix is a pivot column, ${Ax = z}$ is inconsistent and the system has no solution. Thus, ${z}$ is *NOT* in the image of ${T}$.

#### Remark.

Every column except the rightmost column of the augmented matrix (\[A | b]) has a pivot 
${\iff}$ **(a)** every column of the coefficient matrix is a pivot column  
$\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\,$ **(b)** The rightmost column of the augmented matrix is *NOT* a pivot column

>$$\Huge{\iff Ax = b}$$
