### Motivation:

It actually takes time to find the determinant for an ${8 * 8}$ matrix (even a ${4 * 4}$).

Clearly, the determinant computation time grows really fast as the dimension of the matrix goes up!
Indeed, in practice, a cofactor expansion to compute the determinant of an $n * n$ matrix requires about $n!$ floating point operations (or flops).

For instance, when ${n = 25}$,
$$\large \# \;of flops \approx n! = 25! = 1.5 \times 10^{25}$$
Say if a super-computer performs one trillion flops per second, we would have to run it for over *500,000* years to compute the determinant of a $25 * 25$ matrix

___________________________________________________________________
### Question:

Is there an alternative way to compute ${\det(A) = |\,A\,|}$?

To address this question, we need to discuss some properties of determinants

Consider ${\det \left(\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & -1\end{bmatrix} \right) =  \begin{vmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & -1 \end{vmatrix} = 1 \times (-1) - 3 \times 2 = -7}$

Recall: ${\det \left(\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d\end{bmatrix}\right) = \begin{vmatrix} a & b \\ c & d\end{vmatrix} = ad - bc}$

In row reduction (ERO), we'll eliminate 2 below 1

$$\underbrace {\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & -1\end{bmatrix}}_{\huge{A} }\xrightarrow{\huge{R_2\; \to\;R_2\;+\;R_1\;\times\;(-2)}} \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 0 & -7 \end{bmatrix}}_{\huge{B}}$$

By Theorem 1, B is a triangular matrix and
> ${\begin{align}\det&(B) \\ &= \text{product of main diagonals} = 1 * (-7) \\ &=  \large{-7}\end{align}}$
>which is the same as ${\det(A)}$

Recall that Elementary Row Operations: 
>1. Row Addition - Replace one row by adding a multiple of another row
>2. Row Swap - Swap two rows
>3. Row scaling - Multiply one row by a nonzero number k

___________________________________________________________________

If we perform an ERO (row reduction)

 $$\Huge{A \xrightarrow[]{\;\;\;\;\;ERO\;\;\;\;\;} B}$$  
### Question:

How is $\det(B)$ related to $\det(A)$?
What is the relationship between $det(A)$ and $det(B)$?

Indeed, we have the following properties of determinants

___________________________________________________________________
### Theorem 2. 

Let A be a square matrix
>1. If we perform a *row addition* on $A$ to produce a matrix $B$, then $det(B)$ = $det(A)$.
>2. If we perform a *row swap* on $A$ to produce a matrix $B$, then $det(B)$ = $-det(A)$.
>3. If we perform a *row scaling by a factor k* on A to produce a matrix $B$, then $det(B)$ = $kdet(A).$

### Example 5. 
Find the determinant of $A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 1 & -1 \\ 2 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$
#### Solution:

##### Method 1: Cofactor Expansion Along the 1st Column

> ${\begin{align} |\;A\;| &= a_{11}C_{11} + a_{21}C_{21} + a_{31}C_{31} \\ \\ &= +0 \times \begin{vmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{vmatrix} - 1 \times \begin{vmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 0\end{vmatrix} + 2 \times \begin{vmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 1 & -1\end{vmatrix} \\ \\ &= 0 \times (1 \times 0 - 2 \times 1) - 1 (1 \times 0 - 2 \times 1) + 2 \times (1 \times -1 - 2 \times 1) \\ \\ &= 0 \times (-2) - 1 \times (-2) + 2 \times (-3) \\ \\ &= 0 + 2 - 6 \\ \\ &= -4\end{align}}$

##### Method 2: Use Theorem 2 (Properties of Determinants) to compute | A |
$\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;A$
$\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 1 & -1 \\ 2 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$ $\xrightarrow{\huge{R_1\leftrightarrow R_2}}$ $\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$ $\xrightarrow{\huge{R_3\rightarrow R_3 + R_1 \times (-2)}}$ $\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 \\ 0 & -1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$  $\xrightarrow{\huge{R_3\rightarrow R_3 + R_2}}$

$\begin{bmatrix} \fbox{1} & 1 & -1 \\ 0 & \fbox{1} & 2 \\ 0 & 0 & \fbox{4} \end{bmatrix}$ $\large EF!$ (Matrix $\large B$)

Since $B$ is an upper triangular matrix,
>${\begin{align} \det(B) &= \text{\;product\;of\;main\;diagonals} \\ &= 1 \times 1 \times 4 = 4 \end{align}}$
>(By Theorem 1)

Since we use one row swap and two row additions, by Theorem 2
$${\det(B) = -\det(A)}$$
So $\textbf{\large det(A) = -4}$.
This is consistent with the determinant we got by using cofactor expansion!

### Example 6.
Find the determinant of ${A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 2 & 3 & 4 \\ 2 & 4 & 0 & 2 \\ 6 & 0 & 0 & 6 \\ 8 & 6 & 3 & 13 \end{bmatrix}}$
#### Solution:
Use Theorem 2 to find the determinant.

${\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 2 & 3 & 4 \\ 2 & 4 & 0 & 2 \\ 6 & 0 & 0 & 6 \\ 8 & 6 & 3 & 13 \end{bmatrix}}$ $\xrightarrow{\color{red}\huge {R_1\leftrightarrow R_2}}$ ${\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 & 0 & 2 \\ 0 & 2 & 3 & 4 \\ 6 & 0 & 0 & 6 \\ 8 & 6 & 3 & 13 \end{bmatrix}}$ $\xrightarrow{\color{red}\huge{R_1\rightarrow R_1 \times (1/2)}}$ 

${\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 3 & 4 \\ 6 & 0 & 0 & 6 \\ 8 & 6 & 3 & 13 \end{bmatrix}}$ $\xrightarrow[\huge{R_4 \; \rightarrow \; R_4\;+\;R_1\;\times\;(-8)}]{\huge{R_3 \; \rightarrow \; R_3\;+\;R_1\;\times\;(-6)}}$ ${\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 3 & 4 \\ 0 & -12 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -10 & 3 & 5 \end{bmatrix}}$ $\xrightarrow[\huge{R_4 \rightarrow \;R_4\;+\;R_2\;\times\;5}]{\huge{R_3\rightarrow \;R_3\;+\;R_2\;\times\;6}}$ 

${\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 3 & 4 \\ 0 & 0 & 18 & 24 \\ 0 & 0 & 18 & 25 \end{bmatrix}}$ $\xrightarrow[\huge{R_4 \; \rightarrow \; R_4\;+\;R_1\;\times\;(-8)}]{\huge{R_3 \; \rightarrow \; R_3\;+\;R_1\;\times\;(-6)}}$ ${\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix}1 & 2 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 3 & 4 \\ 0 & 0 & 18 & 24 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1\end{bmatrix}}_{\huge{B}}}$

By Theorem 1, B is an upper triangular matrix, ${\det(B)}$ = products of main diagonals = ${ 1 \times 2 \times 18 \times 1}$ 
${=  36}$. Since we use ${\text{\color{red} one swap, one scaling by factor } \frac{1}{2}}$ and several row additions, it follows from Theorem 2 that
$${\det(B) = - \frac{1}{2}} \det(A)$$
Thus, ${\det(A) = - 72}$.

#### Theorem 3.
A square matrix ${A}$ is invertible $\iff$ ${\det(A) \neq 0}$

#### Remark 2.
Now we can add the result in Theorem 3 the IMT as statement 15.

#### Theorem 4. 
Let A and B be two ${n * n}$ matrices. Then
1. ${\det(AB)}) = \det(A)\det(B)$
2. $\large\det(A^T) = \det(A)$

#### Remark 2. 
If A is invertible, then
$${\det(A^{-1})} = \frac{1}{\det(A)}$$
${AA^{-1} = I}$      ${\det(AA^{-1}) = \det(A)\det(A^{-1}) = 1}$

> ${\Rightarrow \det(A^{-1}) = } \frac{ \Huge{1} }{ \Huge{\det(A)} }$

### Example 7. 

Let ${A} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4\end{bmatrix}$, ${B = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 2 \\ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}}$.

#### Solution:

##### Note: 

> $\begin{align}&\large{|\,A\,| = \begin{vmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{vmatrix} = 1 \times 4 - 2 \times 3 = -2} \\ &\large{|\,B\,| = \begin{vmatrix} -1 & 2 \\ 2 & 3 \end{vmatrix} = (-1) \times 3 - 2 \times 2 = -7} \\ &\large{AB = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \times (-1) + 2 \times 2 & 1 \times 2 + 2 \times 3 \\ 3 \times (-1) + 4 \times 2 & 3 \times 2 + 4 \times 3 \end{bmatrix}} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 8 \\ 5 & 18 \end{bmatrix}\end{align}$

Then,

**(1)**

${\begin{align}&|\,AB\,| = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 8 \\ 5 & 18 \end{bmatrix} = 3 \times 18 - 8 \times 5 = 14 \\ &|\,A\;|\;B\,| = (-2) \times (-7) = 14\end{align}}$

>Remark: To compute ${|\,AB\,|}$, it suffices to find ${|\,A\,|}$ and ${|\,B\,|}$, and then use $$ \large{|\,AB\,|\;=\;|\,A\,|\,*\,|\,B\,|}$$

**(2)**

${A^{T} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{bmatrix}^T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix}}$

${\Rightarrow \det(A^T) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix} = 1 \times 4 - 2 \times 2 = -2 = \det(A)}$
#### Remark.
$${|\,A^T\,|\;=\;|\,A\,|}$$
If we know ${|\,A\,|}$, there is no need to compute ${|\,A^T\,|}$.

### Question:
$${\det(A^{-1}) = \frac{1}{\det(A)} = -\frac{1}{2}}$$
$${\det(AB^T) = \det(A) \times \det(B) = \det(A)\det(B) = 14}$$

### Example 8.
In this example, we'll practice properties of determinants.
Let
$${A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ g & h & i\end{bmatrix}}.$$
Suppose ${\det(A) = 3}$. Use the properties of the determinants of the following matrices.

$${(1)\,A_1 = \begin{bmatrix}a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ 2g & 2h & 2i\end{bmatrix},\;(2)\,A_2 = \begin{bmatrix} a+4d & b+4e & c+4f \\ d & e & f \\ g & h & i\end{bmatrix},\;(3)\,A_{3} = \begin{bmatrix} a & d & -g \\ b & e & h \\ c & f & -i \end{bmatrix}}$$
$${(4)\;2A = \begin{bmatrix} 2a & 2b & 2e \\ 2d & 2e & 2f \\ 2g & 2h & 2i \end{bmatrix},\;(5)\;A^3,\;(6)\;A^{-1}}$$
### Solution

**(1)**

>${|\,A_1\,| = 2 \times |\,A\,| = 2 \times 3 = 6}$
>$${\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ g & h & i\end{bmatrix}}_{\huge{A}} \xrightarrow[]{\huge{R_3\;\rightarrow\;R_3\,\times\,2}} \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ 2g & 2h & 2i \end{bmatrix}}_{\huge{A_1}}}$$

**(2)**

>${|\,A_1\,| = |\,A\,| = 3}$ since row addition does *not* affect the determinant
>$${\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ g & h & i\end{bmatrix}}_{\huge{A}} \xrightarrow[]{\huge{R_1\;\rightarrow\;R_1\,+\,4\,\times\,R_2}} \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} a\,+\,4d & b\,+\,4e & c\,+\,4f \\ d & e & f \\ g & h & i\end{bmatrix}}_{\huge{A_2}}}$$

**(3)**

>**Note:**
>**Step 1:** $${\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ g & h & i\end{bmatrix}}_{\huge{A}} \xrightarrow{\huge{R_3\;\rightarrow\;R_3\,\times\,(-1)}} \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ -g & -h & -i\end{bmatrix}}_{\huge{B}}}$$ ${\det(B) = (-1) * \det(A)}$
>**Step 2:** $${\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ -g & -h & -i\end{bmatrix}^T}_{\huge{A}} = \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} a & d & -g \\ b & e & -h \\ c & f & -i\end{bmatrix}}_{\huge{A_3}}}$$ ${\det(B) = \det(B^T)}$

Since ${B^T = A_3}$,
$${\det(A_3) = \det(B^T) = \det(B) = -\det(A) = -3}$$

**(4)** 

${2A = \begin{bmatrix} 2a & 2b & 2c \\ 2d & 2e & 2f \\ 2g & 2h & 2i\end{bmatrix}}$
$${|\,2A\,| = 2 \times 2 \times 2\times|\,A\,| = 2^3 \times|\,A\,| = 2^3 \times 3 = 24}$$
**(5)**

${|\,A^3\,| = (|\,A\,|^3) = 3^3 = 27}$

**(6)**

${|\,A^{-1}\,|\;=\;\frac{\Huge{1}}{\Huge{|\,A\,|}}\;=\;\frac{\Huge{1}}{\Huge{3}}}$

#### Remark:

>${|\,A^{2}\,|\;= \;|\,A\times A\,|\;=\;(\,|\,A\,|\,)^{2}}$
>${|\,A^{3}\,|\;= \;|\,A\times A\,\times\,A|\;=\;(\,|\,A\,|\,)^{3}}$

In general,

>${|\,A^{k}\,|\;= \;|\,A\times A\;... \times\,A|\;=\;(\,|\,A\,|\,)^{2}}$ for ${k\;=\;1,\;2,\;3,\;4\;...}$
