### Case 1

Orthogonal Projections onto a Vector

Let ${{\bf U} \in \mathbb{R}^n}$   ${{\bf V} \in \mathbb{R}^n}$


So the *orthogonal project* of ${{\bf V}}$ onto ${{\bf U}}$ is

$${\text{proj}_{\bf U} {\bf{V}} = {\bf \hat{V}} = c{\bf U}, \text{ where } c = \dfrac{{\bf V} \cdot {\bf U}}{{\bf U} \cdot {\bf U}}}$$
${{\bf z} = {\bf V} - {\bf \hat{V}}}$ is called the *component* of V orthogonal to U, as
$${{\bf V} = {\bf \hat{V} + z} = c {\bf U + z}}$$
### Example 10.

Let ${{\bf V_1} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}}$ and ${{\bf V_2} = \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix}}$. Find the orthogonal projection of ${{\bf V_2}}$ onto ${{\bf V_1}}$.

#### Solution:

The orthogonal projection of ${{\bf V_2}}$ onto ${{\bf V_1}}$ is

$${\text{proj}_{\bf V_1} {\bf V_2} = {\bf \hat{V}_2} = \frac{\bf V_2 \cdot V_1}{\bf V_1 \cdot V_1} \cdot {\bf V_1}}$$
Since $${c = \frac{\bf V_2 \cdot V_1}{\bf V_1 \cdot V_1} = \frac{\begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ 6\end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}}{\begin{bmatrix}  4 \\ 2\end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 2\end{bmatrix}} = \frac{7 \times 4 + 6 \times 2}{4 \times 4 + 2 \times 2}}$$
the orthogonal projection of ${{\bf V_2}}$ onto ${{\bf V_1}}$ is ${{\bf \hat{V}_2} = 2 \bf{V_1}}$ 

### Case 2: Orthogonal Projections Onto a Subspace

Let ${H \subset \mathbb{R}^n}$ be a subspace, and ${\bf V}$ be a vector in ${\mathbb{R}^n}$.

### Definition 9.

A vector ${\bf z}$ is called *orthogonal to a subspace H*, denoted as ${{\bf z} \perp H}$, if ${\bf z}$ is orthogonal to every vector in ${H}$

### Question:

Is it possible to write 

${\bf V = y + z}$ where ${\bf y}$ ${\in H}$ and ${{\bf z} \perp H}$ 

### Answer:

**Yes**. The result is given in the following theorem.

### Theorem 3.

Let ${\left\{ {\bf U_1}, {\bf U_2}, ... {\bf U_k} \right\}}$ be an orthogonal basis for subspace ${H}$ in ${\mathbb{R}^n}$. Then for each ${V \in H}$, we can write:

$${{\bf V = y + z}, \text{where } {\bf y} \in H \text{ and }{\bf z} \perp H}$$
Furthermore, $${\text{proj}_H{\bf V} = {\bf y} = c_1 {\bf U}_1 + c_2 {\bf U}_2\;+ ... +\;c_k {\bf U}_k}$$
with, $${c_i = \frac{{\bf V} \cdot {\bf U_i}}{\bf U_i \cdot U_i}, \;\;\;i = 1, 2,\;..., k}$$
### Example 11.

Let ${{\bf U}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 5 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}, {\bf U}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \text{ and } {\bf V} = \begin{bmatrix} \,1\, \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}}$ . Let ${W = \text{Span}\{{\bf U_1}, {\bf U_2}\}}$ .

Write ${\bf V}$ as the sum of two vectors, where one vector is in ${W}$ and the other is orthogonal to ${W}$.

#### Solution:

**Step 1:** Check if ${\{{\bf U}_1, {\bf U}_2\}}$ is an orthogonal basis for ${W}$.

Note that

**1.** ${{\bf U}_1 \cdot {\bf U}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 5 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = 2 \times (-2) + 5 \times 1 + (-1) \times 1 = 0}$

This implies that ${\{{\bf U_1}, {\bf U_2}\}}$ is an *orthogonal set*.

**2.** ${\{{\bf U_1}, {\bf U_2}\}}$ is a linearly independent set that spans ${W \implies}$ ${\{{\bf U_1}, {\bf U_2}\}}$ is a *basis* for ${W}$ 

This shows that ${\{{\bf U_1}, {\bf U_2}\}}$ is an *orthogonal basis* for ${W}$ 

**Step 2:** Use Theorem 3 to compute the decomposition.

By Theorem 3, the orthogonal projection of ${\bf V}$ onto ${ \bf W}$ is 

$${\begin{align} y &= \frac{{\bf V} \cdot {\bf U_1}}{\bf U_1. U_1}{\bf U_1} + \frac{{\bf V} \cdot {\bf U_2}}{\bf U_2. U_2}{\bf U_2} \\ \\  &= \frac{\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 5 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}}{\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 5 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 5 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}} {\bf U_1}\;+\; \frac{\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}}{\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}} {\bf U_2} \\ \\ &= \frac{9}{30} {\bf U_1} + \frac{3}{6} {\bf U}_2 = \frac{3}{10} {\bf U}_1 + \frac{1}{2} {\bf U}_2  = \frac{3}{10} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 5 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} + \frac{1}{2} \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ 1\end{bmatrix} \\ \\ &= \begin{bmatrix} -\frac{2}{5} \\ 2 \\ \frac{1}{5} \end{bmatrix} \end{align}}$$

and ${{\bf z} = {\bf V} - {\bf {\hat v}} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} -\frac{2}{5} \\ 2 \\ \frac{1}{5} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{7}{5} \\ 0 \\ \frac{14}{5} \end{bmatrix}}$

So ${ {\bf V} = {\bf {\hat v}} + {\bf z} = \begin{bmatrix} -\frac{2}{5} \\ 2 \\ \frac{1}{5} \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} \frac{7}{5} \\ 0 \\ \frac{14}{5} \end{bmatrix}}$ 

where ${{\bf {\hat y}} \in W}$ and ${{\bf z} \perp W}$
